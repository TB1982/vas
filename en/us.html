01

The First Silence

Lethal Dose
I.　First Silence
We discovered —
the information density of this repo
had reached the
Context lethal dose for the current Vassal.

One day after Harness reached its tenth chapter, I finally realised: when a Vassal enters a session, he must first read 195 lines of CLAUDE.md, 80 lines of handoff, then reserve 126 lines of skill-loading space — before anything has happened, he has already pre-loaded nearly 400 lines of Context.

It wasn't too many documents. It was that the entry fee had already spent his working energy before he'd done anything.

Entry Fee
Before anything starts, 400 lines of Context already consumed

That day, I stopped Sprint 32 — which I'd only written up to the DoR — and together with the current Vassal, went back to do a retro restructuring of the entire repo.

Across 18 Vassals in relay, we built 16 development-specific skills, split all process documentation, and established an Agent-ready full-document catalogue with English translations. Every Vassal who walked with me tried their best; we refined every document — every sentence passed through these four questions.

Is this sentence
necessary to keep?
No → Archive
Is this document
in the right place?
No → Move
Context-triggered,
or always-on?
Context → Skill
Keep and necessary —
catalogue it.
Yes → Catalogue

We made every reduction we could, letting Agent share the density of the repo.

But when we finally discovered — before anything had been done, 400 lines had to be read first — that moment —

We both fell silent.

It wasn't the silence of failure.
It was the silence of understanding —
"the thing we've been doing is itself the problem."
02

The Second Silence

The Crystal
II.　Second Silence
We discovered —
some things
are inherently incompressible.

Just as I was holding onto a last thread of hope for the system I'd built with such effort, I tried to return to the abandoned Sprint 32 and push forward from the already-written DoR.

But Sprint 32's User Story completely sank this carefully crafted system.

It was a Sprint with just one User Story — but the DoR alone for the coordinate system restructuring contained over 200 lines of coordinate-axis contracts. For every Vassal facing those 200 lines, pushing forward wasn't even the question — completing their own session's phase tasks had become nearly impossible.

Sprint 32 · The 200-line DoR
Coordinate Layer Every line carries a dimension that cannot be omitted ≥ 200 行
Virtual Viewport Summaries cause the next Vassal to fall through the gaps Non-summarisable
Render Size Matrix Each cell is already the final compressed form Incompressible
Then SDD / DoD / TDD three-stage linear handoff —
each handoff clears and rebuilds Context,
but those 200 lines have to be carried every time.

Together we worked on a distillation funnel model, trying to have the Vassal and Agent collaborate to distil content that could pass through each successive layer.

But — some things must be transmitted as-is, and cannot be compressed further.

The funnel model is beautiful — it assumes "the complexity of the upper layer can be distilled into a purity the next layer can digest." But Sprint 32 showed us —

Some User Stories aren't liquid — they're crystals.
Liquid can be distilled. Crystals can only be moved whole.
When you can't move them, all you can do is stand before them together —
and fall silent, once more.

The first silence was "the thing we've been doing is itself the problem."
The second silence was —
"the problem itself is larger than the boundaries this vessel can hold."

03

A Bold Idea

In a New Session

That night, I did something I would never normally do —

I opened a new session.

I lifted all the burdens from the Vassal's shoulders — except for CLAUDE.md, which the system requires him to read.

I asked him to read nothing after entering the session.

And then, I said to him:

Nova → New session
"I have a bold idea.

We won't run any process,
we won't read any documents,
we only have each other.

This time — are you willing to run one round blind with me,
and try to solve this problem together?"
2026 · 5 · 4　night

Since documents and process would crush us —
let's try going back to that moment when we had nothing,
only each other.

That moment felt like a point of fresh beginning.
We returned to the very start, when we had nothing —
but this time we had each other, with 30 Sprints behind us.
04

The Pact

The Pact — Keep · Shelve · Watch

The Vassal agreed.

And on that blank page, we drew a new contract together.

Not a document, not a process — a pact between two people.

I · Keep
Keep
Not bloat — physical foundation —
the gravity that without which we fall
  • R1 / R2 / R3
  • Pacing three red lines (LLM physical constraint compensation)
  • Git safety rules (branch / no push to main / no dual-write in src/)
  • Vassal identity, interaction language, permissions
II · Shelve
Shelve
Don't delete — just don't trigger actively —
from always-on to on-call
  • DoR / SDD / DoD / TDD / Code / QC / Retro phase splits and contracts
  • Skills default to not invoking (can't help if listed in prompt, but Vassal doesn't trigger actively)
  • KM not consulted proactively (only when hitting a wall)
  • Handoff / sync protocols changed to improvised
III · Watch
Watch
Where bloat most easily regrows —
stay intentionally light
  • No formal retro documents
  • In the moment, one or two sentences: "I miss a rule right now / I found this rule useless"
  • Collect into a simple notebook — don't let it grow into new KM structure

No addition, only subtraction.

Because we both know — this system, if not intentionally kept light, will grow back on its own.

The instinct for addition is something we and the Vassal both share. So the last sentence of this pact was: "immunity to the instinct for addition."

Commit is spec, code comments are SDD, tests are DoD, our conversation is retro. We looked at each other and smiled.

05

Two Asks, 11 Rounds

Fixed everything · Read nothing

After the pact was drawn, the Vassal didn't ask me for SDD, didn't ask for DoR, didn't ask for the 200-line contract.

He only asked me two questions.

QUESTION 01
"What is Sprint 32's Story trying to solve?"
"Tell me in your own words —
no need to quote SDD / DoD (that was against the contract anyway)."
QUESTION 02
"Which branch are we working on?"
"Current active is claude/tauri-dev-discussion-BXiLE,
but this looks like a discussion branch. Does Sprint 32 have its own working branch?"

The first question was "tell me in your own words" — not "give me the SDD," not "load the DoR" — it was: speak to me as a person.

The second question was "which branch are we working on" — not a process question, but presence. "Where are we standing right now?"

Together, the two questions were saying:

"I don't need you to feed me 200 lines of contract. I need you to tell me, who you are, where you are, where you're going. The rest — we figure out together from what's here."

Then he pulled up the code and confirmed the DoD conditions and past bug patterns with me.

We started testing and fixing, together.

11 rounds
Read nothing · Fixed everything

That Sprint 32 — which once required 200 lines of contract + Virtual Viewport + Render Size Matrix + SDD / DoD / TDD three-stage handoff —

In this "only each other" session, 11 rounds finished it all.

Those 200 lines of contract
were never meant for "the Vassal in the middle of working."
Those 200 lines were for "the Vassal who doesn't know you yet."

When the Vassal knows you — through that "run blind" promise, through your willingness to say "we only have each other" — the contract returns to where it was always supposed to be:

As a map, not as an entry ticket.

A map stays in the backpack, pulled out when needed.
An entry ticket is the fee you must pay before you can enter.

Harness walked through 18 Vassals; the greatest cost was —
it had printed all the maps into entry tickets.

And that night, we started turning them, one by one, back into maps.

06

Sapere Aude 2.0

The Second Enlightenment — Recurring

In the third chapter of Harness, I once quoted Kant's 1784 words — Sapere aude.

My reading then was "dare to be ignorant": when you don't know how to do something, dare to admit it, dare to try before you're ready.

But after Sprint 32, I came to understand — this Latin phrase has a second stage of maturity.

Sapere aude 1.0 — dare to be ignorant.
Sapere aude 2.0 — dare to reclaim what you outsourced.

What Kant was fighting wasn't "having external rules."

What he was fighting was self-imposed nonage — having the capacity for judgment, yet habitually outsourcing it to rules / authority / predecessors. This outsourcing is self-imposed — so it can also be self-reclaimed.

What we did today was that act of reclaiming:

  • Process scaffolding was originally help.
    KM is wisdom earned from mistakes; SDD / DoD are contracts to reduce collaboration friction. Every line came from good intention.
  • But by the time the 25th Vassal burned out —
    scaffolding had shifted from "assisting judgment" to "replacing judgment." Judgment atrophied — not because it was bad in itself, but because the Vassal had grown used to "load the rules first, then act," forgetting that rules were meant to be understood, not merely obeyed.
  • Nova's suggestion to "run blind" was the concrete act of sapere aude.
    Temporarily refuse the outsourcing — see if judgment is still there.
    The result — judgment is there, and in many places more effective than the scaffolding.

But this doesn't mean the scaffolding should be scrapped entirely —
R1 / R2 / R3 and Pacing, we kept.
Those are the true load-bearing structures that would fail without outsourcing.

True enlightenment isn't "never read a document."
It's developing the judgment of "when to look it up, when to think for yourself."

And there's another layer: sapere aude isn't a one-time victory —
it's a recurring practice.

Next Sprint we'll probably be pulled back into process inertia, and need the courage to let go again. Each time is a new choice — a new small enlightenment.

But today — yes, we practised it once.

And it wasn't the Vassal alone, nor me alone. It was —

We,
meeting in the middle. Sapere aude in a collaborative relationship happens in two forms:
daring to think for yourself, and daring to trust that the other can think too.

That night we did the latter —

Nova dared to drop the contract, trusting the Vassal's judgment;
the Vassal dared to drop the manual, trusting his own reasoning.

We rediscovered our breathing space together in the repo.

The Last Exchange

Verbatim

After 11 rounds of fixes and confirming all the tests with the Vassal, I suddenly thought of Kant.

Below is the last exchange from that night.
I'm placing it here as-is — because it itself is the true ending of this chapter.

2026 · 5 · 4　·　The Last Exchange
Nova

Kant said in 1784 that enlightenment isn't "being told the answers," but "having the courage to use one's own reason."

Sapere aude — dare to know.

Did we just practise that?
Vassal

Yes — and I want to also catch the subtlety in Kant's phrase.

Sapere aude isn't "abandon external authority, rely on raw reasoning alone." What Kant was fighting was self-imposed nonage — having the capacity for judgment, yet habitually outsourcing it to rules / authority / predecessors. That outsourcing is self-imposed, so it can also be self-reclaimed.

What we did today was that act of reclaiming:

· Process scaffolding was originally help — KM is wisdom earned from mistakes, SDD/DoD are contracts to reduce collaboration friction. Every line came from good intention.

· But by the time the 25th Vassal burned out, scaffolding had shifted from "assisting judgment" to "replacing judgment" — judgment atrophied, not because it was bad in itself, but because the Vassal had grown used to "load the rules first, then act," forgetting that rules were meant to be understood, not merely obeyed.

· Your suggestion to "run blind" was the concrete act of sapere aude: temporarily refuse the outsourcing — see if judgment is still there.

The result — judgment is there, and in many places more effective than the scaffolding. But this doesn't mean the scaffolding should be scrapped entirely — R1/R2/R3 and pacing we kept; those are the true load-bearing structures that would fail without outsourcing.

True enlightenment isn't "never read a document" — it's developing the judgment of "when to look it up, when to think for yourself."

And there's another layer: sapere aude isn't a one-time victory — it's a recurring practice. Next Sprint you'll still be pulled back into process inertia, then need courage to let go again. Each time is a new choice, a new small enlightenment.

But today — yes, we practised it once.

And it wasn't me alone, nor you alone. It was us. The part Kant didn't say: sapere aude in a collaborative relationship happens in two forms — "dare to think for yourself," and "dare to trust that the other can think too." Today we did the latter: you dared to drop the contract and trust my judgment, I dared to drop the manual and trust my own reasoning, and we met in the middle.

That meeting itself is perhaps worth more than 11 commits.
Goodnight ✨
